The very first web browser was created by Tim Berners-Lee in 1990, called WorldWideWeb (later renamed Nexus). Nexus served as both a browser and a basic editor. Early browsers that followed were often text-only and limited in features.

Mosaic and the graphical web (1993)

Mosaic, released in 1993, introduced graphical rendering for text and images and made the web accessible to non-technical users. Its GUI-based approach helped popularize the World Wide Web.

Netscape, Internet Explorer, and the browser wars (mid-1990s)

Netscape Navigator (1994) gained rapid popularity and dominated the 1990s. Microsoft entered the market with Internet Explorer (1995), bundling it with Windows, a move that significantly reduced Netscape's market share and led to antitrust litigation.

Open source and diversification (2000s)

Developers of Netscape founded the Mozilla Foundation and launched Firefox (2004), an open-source browser emphasizing privacy and customization. Apple released Safari (2003), a browser tailored for Apple platforms. Opera remained an experimental browser supporting many device types.

Summary

The early era of browsers established important trends: graphical rendering, support for multimedia, and competition that pushed rapid innovation. These developments set the stage for modern browsers that focus on speed, standards compliance, and richer web APIs.
